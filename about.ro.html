        SPS Theology

        A rule-governed reference translation that preserves the source where standard English would close it.

        SPS Theology — Source-Preserving Scripture — is a translation methodology and a working translation built on it. The methodology rests on a single conviction: when standard English Bibles render every Hebrew or Greek term with a familiar English equivalent, they perform an act of interpretation that the reader cannot see. SPS makes those acts of interpretation visible.

        The methodology recognises three rendering modes. Mode 1 is plain English, used when a target-language word carries the source's full sense without distortion. Mode 2 renders the English equivalent but documents the source term inline through the apparatus notation {— source-term}, signalling that the rendering is an approximation. Mode 3 retains the source term in transliterated form because no English equivalent preserves the original's morphological complexity, intertextual range, or grammatical openness.

        Five steps govern the choice between modes for any given term. The decision procedure asks whether the term is structurally significant in its co-text; whether co-textual factors disambiguate its semantic range; whether a single English equivalent preserves the source's full lexical scope; whether translation conventions in the target tradition have already overdetermined the rendering; and whether reception history has accumulated dogmatic content that the source-language did not yet carry. A term passing all five steps is rendered plainly. A term failing any of them moves toward Mode 2 or Mode 3, with the procedure's audit trail visible in the side panel for any reader to inspect.

        Why this matters

        Every translation makes thousands of micro-decisions. Standard English Bibles present those decisions as the text — the reader sees only the English, with no indication of the choices that produced it. SPS treats decisions as decisions and exposes them. For someone wanting to read Scripture exegetically, this is a different kind of relationship between translator and reader: not a curated experience, but a transparent one.

        SPS is not a devotional translation. The reading view is denser than the King James, the New International, or any of their relatives. Mode 3 transliterations slow the eye. Apparatus markers invite clicks. The translation will not preach itself. It is built for the reader who wants to study what the source actually says and is willing to do the work of attending to it.

        How to read the site

        The reading view shows a book and a division of that book — for example, Bereshit D1, which is the first toledot of Genesis (1:1–2:3). Within the reading column, three kinds of markup signal active apparatus.

        A word styled with a dotted underline and an inline notation like archē {— ἀρχή} is a Mode 2 documented rendering. Click it: the side panel opens with the term's gloss, an explanatory note, the five-step decision procedure showing which steps the rendering passed and failed, and the methodological axioms it serves.

        An italicised word with a coloured underline like Elohim is a Mode 3 transliteration — a term whose source has been retained because English cannot carry its full sense. Click it for the same kind of apparatus.

        An ambiguity marker, shown as [[a | b]] or [[◊]], signals a place where the source-language is genuinely open between two or more readings. Click it to see all readings the source permits, with the grammatical or text-critical reasoning behind the openness.

        The Legend page documents these conventions in detail.

        Where the project stands

        The Hebrew Bible is being built outward from Genesis. The Greek New Testament from the Gospel of John. Each book is its own module — once a book is registered, its apparatus is available to commentaries and cross-references throughout the site. Romanian translations of the apparatus and reading content are being added passage by passage.

        The project is intended as a long-running scholarly resource rather than a finished publication. New passages, new commentaries, and refinements to existing renderings are added as work proceeds.

        On the name

        SPS stands for Source-Preserving Scripture. The phrase names what the translation does: it preserves the source where target-language renderings would collapse it. The brand mark {—} is the apparatus notation itself — every Mode 2 rendering on the site uses this exact bracket-and-dash form to mark a documented translation, so the brand is a sample of the methodology it represents.
