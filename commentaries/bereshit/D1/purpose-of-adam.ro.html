        The purpose of adam in Bereshit D1

        On tselem, demut, and yirdu: what the first toledot says humanity is for.

        When Bereshit D1 introduces humanity in verse 26, it does not introduce persons. It introduces a function. The Hebrew sequence is precise — tselem, demut, and the verb yirdu — and the three terms together describe what ha-adam is summoned into being to do. Standard English translations let "image," "likeness," and "have dominion over" carry weight that long centuries of theological reception have loaded into them. The Hebrew is doing something more specific.

        The text in question

        Verse 26 of the first toledot reads:

        Bereshit D1 · Gen 1:26 And Elohim said, "Let us make ha-adam in our tselem, after our demut, and let them yirdu over the fish of the sea, and over the birds of the shamaim, and over the cattle, and over all the earth, and over every creeping thing that creeps upon the earth."

        Three terms, one purpose statement. The grammar is causal: ha-adam is made in tselem and after demut so that the dominion-relation can hold. The image-language is not decorative; it is the warrant for the function.

        What tselem actually means

        The standard English "image" comes through the Latin imago and carries with it sixteen centuries of theological elaboration — the imago Dei tradition that runs from Augustine through Aquinas through the Reformers to modern systematic theology. By the time a modern reader sees "image of God," the term carries content the Hebrew did not yet bear: substantive personhood, rational soul, moral capacity, freedom of will.

        The Hebrew tselem is far more concrete. In its other biblical occurrences, tselem denotes a physical statue or relief — the cast images of Baal in 2 Kings 11:18, the gold tumours and mice the Philistines made in 1 Samuel 6:5, the bronze images of Nebuchadnezzar in Daniel. The Akkadian cognate ṣalmu is used throughout the ancient Near East for the royal image-statue: a king's representative, set up in distant provinces to make the king's rule present where the king himself was not.

        This is what the term meant in its compositional horizon. To say that ha-adam is made betselem Elohim is to say that humanity is the royal-statue of the Creator — placed in the world the way an Assyrian king would place his image in a conquered province, not because the statue resembles the king but because it represents him, makes his authority present.

        And demut?

        The second term is demut, conventionally rendered "likeness." Where tselem is the statue itself, demut indicates the relationship of resemblance or correspondence between two things — a likeness, a representation, a model.

        The two terms function as a hendiadys: a single conceptual unit expressed through two related words. Tselem alone might suggest mere placement (the statue is here); demut qualifies it (the statue resembles its referent). Together they say: ha-adam is set in the world as the Creator's representative, in a relation of correspondence with the One represented. The "likeness" is functional and relational — what makes the representation work — not a list of substantive properties humanity supposedly shares with the divine nature.

        The verb yirdu

        The verb yirdu — usually translated "have dominion" or "rule" — is the operative claim. The image-language exists so that ha-adam can perform this function. Standard English "dominion" carries colonial and exploitative connotations from its long political history. The Hebrew root r-d-h means to administer, to govern, to exercise responsible authority. It is the verb used of a shepherd over a flock, of a wise king over a kingdom, of a steward over an estate. It is administrative, not rapacious.

        What ha-adam is for, in the structure of the first toledot, is to be the Creator's administrator over the rest of creation. The image is the warrant; the dominion is the function.

        The implication: humanity as priestly-administrative

        Combining the three terms, the picture that emerges is unmistakably priestly-administrative. Ha-adam is created to be the Creator's image-bearing presence on earth — placed in the world as the royal statue is placed in the province, charged with administering the realm on behalf of the One whose image it bears.

        This connects forward to the second toledot in Bereshit D2, where ha-adam is placed in the garden and given the task of avad-shamar — to serve and to keep. Those two verbs are, elsewhere in the Hebrew Bible, technical terms for the priestly service of the Tabernacle. The garden is being framed as a sanctuary; ha-adam is being framed as its priest. The priestly function announced in D1 is given concrete expression in D2.

        What this changes for reading the rest of Genesis

        Once humanity's purpose is set this way — as the Creator's image-bearing administrator in a creation framed as sanctuary — the narrative shape of Genesis 3 onward becomes legible as the disruption of this specific function. The transgression is not primarily a moral failure in the abstract. It is a failure of administrative trust, a refusal of the priestly vocation, a forfeit of the role for which ha-adam was made. The expulsion from the garden is the loss of the sanctuary; the placement of the keruvim at the entrance is the replacement of the priestly humanity with cherub-guardians — the same figures that flank the Ark in the Tabernacle and Temple, marking divine presence as inaccessible.

        The whole arc that follows — Cain, the flood, Bavel, Avram — is the question of how the priestly-administrative function, lost in Eden, is to be recovered. Israel's vocation as a "kingdom of priests" (Exodus 19:6) is the answer the Torah eventually gives: where humanity in general failed, this people will carry the function. And the New Testament will, on its own terms, take the question further, claiming in 1 Peter 2:9 that those who are in Christos form a "royal priesthood" — the recovered humanity. But the question itself is set in Bereshit D1: what is ha-adam for?

        A word on what is not said

        It is worth noting what Bereshit D1 does not say about humanity. It does not say humanity has an immortal soul. It does not say humanity is metaphysically distinct from the animals (ha-adam and the animals share the designation nephesh chayyah, "living being," with no morphological difference). It does not say humanity is rational where animals are not. These are claims that have been read into the text from later philosophical and theological sources.

        What Bereshit D1 says about humanity is functional, relational, and administrative: ha-adam is made in tselem, after demut, in order to yirdu. The purpose is the image; the image is the purpose. Once that is heard, the rest of the Hebrew Bible reads differently.
