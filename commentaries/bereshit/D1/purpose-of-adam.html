        The Purpose of adam in Bereshit D1

        A forensic reading of tselem, demut, yirdu, and kivshuha: the cosmic investiture of humanity.

        Terminology note

        Throughout this commentary, adam (uncapitalized, following the Hebrew convention where no capital letters exist) denotes humanity as a collective — the human species, mankind. ha-adam ("the adam") denotes the human individual or the human as a specific referent, depending on context. Where the Hebrew text uses the definite article, the commentary does the same. The divine name is rendered YHWH (capitalized), following the SPS convention of preserving the tetragrammaton rather than substituting "LORD." All other transliterated Hebrew terms appear in lowercase — elohim, tselem, demut, yirdu, kivshuha — echoing the uncial script of the source language.

        When Bereshit D1 introduces humanity in p20-22 (Gen 1:26-28), it does not introduce persons in the modern psychological sense. It introduces a function — a cosmic office established by divine decree. To understand what is happening, we must recover the Ancient Near Eastern legal, ritual, and ontological frameworks that give these terms their original technical weight. This is a jurisdictional text, not a philosophical one.

        The Decree

        Bereshit D1 · Gen 1:26-28 Then elohim said: "Let us make adam in our tselem and according to our demut; and let them yirdu over the fish of the sea, the birds of the shamaim, the cattle, the whole earth, and every creeping thing."
        So elohim created ha-adam in his tselem; in the tselem of elohim he created him; male and female he created them.
        And elohim blessed them; and elohim said to them: "Be fruitful and multiply; fill the earth and kivshuha; and yirdu over every living thing that moves on the earth."

        1. The Divine Council (na'aseh): A Matter of Cosmic Law

        The phrase "Let us make" is not a rhetorical flourish, nor is it a veiled reference to later Trinitarian doctrine. In its own Ancient Near Eastern context, it references the Divine Assembly — the heavenly host that serves as the Creator's court.

        We see this council operating throughout the Hebrew Bible. Tehilim D82.p1 (Psalms 82:1) declares that "elohim stands in the divine assembly; in the midst of the elohim he holds judgment." Job 1:6 describes a day when "the sons of elohim came to present themselves before YHWH." The prophet Micaiah ben Imlah sees "YHWH sitting on his throne, and all the host of heaven standing beside him" (1 Kings 22:19). Isaiah witnesses the seraphim attending the throne (Isaiah 6). This is the governmental structure of the heavens in the biblical imagination.

        Forensic Context: By consulting this council, elohim makes the creation of adam a matter of recorded cosmic statute. This is not a private act of divine craftsmanship; it is a public investiture before the entire spiritual government. adam is introduced to the heavenly powers not merely as a creature among creatures, but as a newly appointed dignitary — a representative whose authority derives from and reports to this higher jurisdiction.

        Implication: adam is vetted and acknowledged by the heavens. adam's authority on earth is not self-generated; it is delegated, witnessed, and accountable. The creation of adam (humanity) is, from its first moment, a legal event within a cosmic governmental structure.

        This forensic framing is essential for everything that follows in Genesis. When the nahash approaches the woman in Bereshit D2.p21 (Genesis 3:1), the question is not merely whether she will eat a piece of fruit. The question is whether adam (humanity) will remain loyal to the jurisdiction that appointed it, or whether it will defect to a rival authority. The entire drama of Bereshit D2.p21–D8 (Genesis 3–11) — qayin, the flood, bavel — is a legal drama about covenantal fidelity within a cosmic government.

        2. tselem (Image): The Living Cult Statue

        The standard English "image" comes through the Latin imago and carries with it sixteen centuries of theological elaboration — the imago Dei tradition running from Irenaeus through Augustine through Aquinas through the Reformers to modern systematic theology. By the time a modern reader sees "image of God," the term has accumulated content the Hebrew did not yet bear: substantive personhood, rational soul, moral capacity, freedom of will — a list of abstract properties supposedly distinguishing humans from animals.

        The Hebrew tselem is far more concrete. In its other biblical occurrences, tselem consistently denotes a physical statue or relief — a manufactured object that represents something else: the cast images of Baal torn down in 2 Kings 11:18, the gold tumours and mice the Philistines made as guilt offerings in 1 Samuel 6:5, the bronze image of Nebuchadnezzar's dream in Daniel 2.

        The Akkadian cognate ṣalmu provides the decisive cultural context. Throughout Mesopotamia in the second and first millennia BC, a ṣalmu was the royal image-statue: a physical representation of the king, manufactured and placed in distant provinces or conquered territories. The purpose was not decorative. The statue made the king's rule present where the king himself was not physically located.

        ANE background

        The Akkadian term ṣalmu (cognate to Hebrew tselem) appears in Neo-Assyrian royal inscriptions with the specific sense of "royal statue" — a physical representation of the king placed in a distant province to mark it as territory under his dominion. The statue was not a likeness in the sense of portraiture; it was a presence. When Genesis takes this term and applies it to adam in relation to the Creator, it makes an ANE-political claim about what humanity is for: to be the Creator's governing presence in creation.

        The Presence Mechanism: The statue was the legal embodiment of the sovereign's presence and authority. To honor the statue was to honor the king. To deface the statue was to declare rebellion against the king. The statue was a territorial marker that established the king's claim over that region.

        The Shift in Bereshit: Here, Bereshit takes this familiar ANE practice and performs a radical reconfiguration. The "statue" is not made of wood, stone, or metal. The statue is living flesh. ha-adam is the animated cult image of elohim, planted on Earth to make the invisible sovereignty of YHWH visible, tangible, and operational within the material realm.

        Forensic Purpose: The tselem grants territorial rights. Where the image is placed, the king's claim is established. Earth is not neutral territory; it is claimed territory. adam's very existence on the planet is the Creator's declaration that this realm belongs to Him. This is not about adam resembling YHWH elohim in some abstract sense; it is about adam (humanity) representing YHWH — making divine authority present on earth, the way an Assyrian king's image made royal authority present in a distant province.

        Therefore, adam (humanity) is not a passive recipient of divine attributes; adam is an active agent of divine governance. The image is not a static status; it is a dynamic function. To be the tselem of elohim is to be the living, governing presence of the Creator on Earth — the one through whom the Creator's will is executed and whose actions carry the weight of divine authority.

        3. demut (Likeness): The Internal Warrant of Authority

        If tselem is the external "statue" — the visible, physical placement of the representative — demut is the internal charter that backs the statue's claim.

        Genetic/Legal Link: In Ancient Near Eastern genealogies, a son who is in his father's demut carries his father's lineage and legal standing. Genesis 5:3 uses this exact term when it says that adam "fathered a son in his own likeness (bidmuto), after his image (ketsalmo)." The language of tselem and demut is therefore not unique to humanity's relationship to YHWH; it is the normal language of sonship, inheritance, and legal succession.

        The two terms — tselem and demut — function as a hendiadys: a single conceptual unit expressed through two related words. tselem alone might suggest mere physical placement (the statue is here); demut qualifies it by specifying the relationship between the statue and the one it represents.

        Forensic Interpretation: demut signifies inherited operational capacity. It is the "YHWH-like" cognitive and moral endowment — the capacity for reason, moral judgment, creative speech, and covenantal relationship — that enables adam to execute his vice-regal duties in a manner consistent with the Sovereign's character. A son sent to manage his father's estate does not need to be his father; he needs to carry his father's authority and to act in a manner consistent with his father's interests and character. demut is the warrant that makes the tselem's claim legitimate and operational.

        4. yirdu (Rule) and kivshuha (Subdue): The Scope of the Commission

        The verb yirdu — usually translated "have dominion" or "rule" — is the operative claim. The entire image-language exists so that ha-adam can perform this function. The purpose is the image; the image is the purpose.

        yirdu: This verb denotes the rule of a shepherd-king — authority exercised for the benefit and good order of the governed. It is stewardship, not exploitation. The image of the shepherd is crucial: a shepherd rules the flock not for his own benefit but for theirs. He leads them to pasture, protects them from predators, and ensures their flourishing. His authority is real, but it is for the flock, not against it. Standard English "dominion" has accumulated colonial and exploitative connotations from centuries of empire that the Hebrew verb does not carry.

        Translation hazard

        The English "dominion" (KJV, NRSV) and "rule" (NIV, ESV) both import political connotations that the Hebrew r-d-h does not intrinsically carry. In the Hebrew Bible, this root governs shepherds over flocks (Ezek 34:4), priests over the sanctuary (Lev 25:43, 46, 53 — where the same root appears in the context of how one may not rule harshly), and the ideal king over a peaceable realm (1 Kgs 5:4, of Solomon). The register is custodial governance, not extraction or domination.

        kivshuha: This is a land-grant term, meaning literally to "bring under the heel." It acknowledges that the Earth is a raw, untamed estate — not yet the ordered sanctuary it is destined to become. The command implies a transformational task: humanity is to cultivate the wildness, to bring order out of chaos, until the entire planet reflects the order, beauty, and fruitfulness of the Garden-sanctuary — its intended "Edenic state."

        The two verbs together define the full scope of the commission. yirdu is the ongoing governance — the continuous exercise of shepherd-king authority. kivshuha is the initial and continuing work of transformation — bringing the raw material of creation to its full flourishing under divine sovereignty. Humanity is placed on Earth as the Creator's administrative presence, charged with both governing and cultivating the realm.

        5. The "Male and Female" Necessity: Completing the tselem

        D.p21 (verse 27) specifies that ha-adam was created "male and female." This is not an afterthought or a secondary detail. It is a forensic necessity for a complete representation of the Creator's governance.

        The Divine Council as Plurality: The "us" of p20 (verse 26) implies relational plurality within unity at the level of divine governance. The Divine Council operates through relationship, collaboration, and differentiated roles within a unified purpose. A singular, isolated human could not iconographically reflect this reality. A solitary human would be a defective image — a partial representation that fails to communicate the relational nature of the appointing authority.

        tselem as Relational Entity: In Ancient Near Eastern diplomatic practice, treaties between nations were often symbolized by paired statues — two figures standing together, representing the covenant between two parties. Male and female together become the living icon of covenant relationship. They model differentiation-in-unity: two distinct persons, with distinct capacities and perspectives, bound together in a collaborative purpose that neither could fulfill alone.

        Full-Spectrum Representation: The duality of male and female allows humanity to embody a fuller spectrum of divine attributes — strength and nurture, justice and mercy, initiation and reception. Their "one flesh" union (D2.p20 / Genesis 2:24) becomes the fundamental operational unit for fulfilling the dominion mandate: the basic cell of the colony of heaven on earth.

        The blessing "be fruitful and multiply" is therefore not merely biological. It is administrative. To fill the earth with image-bearers is to fill the earth with the Creator's representative presence — to replicate the colony until the entire territory is governed. Every new human being is a new outpost of divine administration, a new living statue extending the Creator's claim across the untamed earth.

        6. Synthesis: The Priestly-Administrative Vocation

        Combining all these elements, the picture that emerges is unmistakably priestly-administrative. ha-adam is created to be the Creator's tselem-bearing presence on earth — placed in the world as the royal statue is placed in the province, equipped with the inherited operational capacity (demut) to function effectively, and charged with administering the realm (yirdu) and transforming its rawness into ordered flourishing (kivshuha).

        This connects directly to the second toledot in Bereshit D2, where ha-adam is placed in the garden and given the task of avad and shamar — "to serve and to keep." Those two verbs are, elsewhere in the Hebrew Bible, technical terms for the priestly service of the Tabernacle. The priests "serve" (avad) in the sanctuary and "keep" (shamar) its ordinances and boundaries (Numbers 3:7-8; 8:26; 18:5-6). The garden is being framed as a sanctuary; ha-adam is being framed as its priest. The cosmic-administrative function announced in D1 is given concrete, local expression in D2.

        7. What This Changes for Reading the Rest of the Biblical Narrative

        Once humanity's purpose is set this way — as the Creator's tselem-bearing administrator in a creation framed as sanctuary — the narrative shape of Bereshit D2.p21 (Genesis 3) onward becomes legible as the disruption of this specific function and the long arc of its recovery.

        The Transgression as Administrative Failure: The transgression in Bereshit D2 (Genesis 3) is not primarily a moral failure in the abstract — a violation of an arbitrary rule. It is a failure of administrative trust, a refusal of the priestly vocation, a forfeit of the office for which ha-adam was made. The nahash approaches the woman not with a temptation to generic sin but with a challenge to the jurisdictional structure itself: "Did elohim really say…?" The question is designed to undermine trust in the appointing authority. The transgression is a breach of covenantal fidelity within the cosmic government.

        The Expulsion as Loss of Sanctuary: The expulsion from the garden is the loss of the sanctuary — the forfeiture of the priestly station. The placement of the keruvim at the garden's entrance is devastatingly precise: these guardian beings, familiar from Ancient Near Eastern iconography as composite winged creatures that flank divine thrones, now replace the priestly humanity. The same figures that guard the Ark of the Covenant in the Tabernacle and Temple now mark divine presence as inaccessible to the very beings who were made to dwell in it.

        The Arc of Recovery: The whole narrative arc that follows — qayin's murder and exile, the violence leading to the flood, the scattering at bavel, the call of avram — is the question of how the priestly-administrative function, lost in eden, is to be recovered.

        Israel's vocation as a "kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:6) is the answer the Torah eventually gives: where humanity in general failed, this particular people will carry the function. They will be the Creator's tselem-bearing administrators in microcosm, a "light to the nations" (Isaiah 42:6) that models what restored humanity looks like. The Tabernacle and later the Temple become the restored garden-sanctuary, with the Levitical priesthood standing in the place of ha-adam. The entire priestly system of the Torah is a temporary reinstatement of the original human vocation, administered by a particular tribe until the function can be restored to humanity as a whole.

        The New Testament will take the question further. 1 Peter 2:9 declares that those who are in christos form a "royal priesthood" — the recovered humanity, the original tselem-bearing community restored to its administrative function. Paul's language of the "new adam" (1 Corinthians 15:45; Romans 5:12-21) operates within this same framework: christos is the one who succeeds where the first adam failed, and those united to him participate in the restored vocation.

        But the question itself is set in Bereshit D1, before there is sin, before there is redemption, before there is Israel or Church or eschatology. There is only this: What is humanity for? And the answer is: to be the Creator's living tselem — the visible presence of invisible sovereignty — the administrator of a creation destined to become a sanctuary, the representation of his Creator's character.

        What Is Not Said

        It is worth noting — clearly and emphatically — what Bereshit D1 and the creation account of D2 do not say about adam (humanity). Many concepts that later theology has considered central to the doctrine of humanity are simply absent from the Bereshit text:

        • It does not say humanity has an immortal soul. ha-adam is constituted from the aphar and returns to the aphar. The nephesh is not an immaterial substance that survives death; it is the living self, the unified breathing-desiring-acting being. Resurrection in the Hebrew Bible is not the release of a soul from a body but the reconstitution of the whole person by divine power (for this see the commentary on Bereshit D2.p20 / Genesis 2:7).
        • It does not say humanity is rational where animals are not. The capacity for discernment, moral judgment, and covenantal relationship — what demut signifies — is framed as an inherited operational capacity for the administrative task, not a metaphysical property that defines human "substance."
        • It does not ground human dignity in any inherent quality. What gives humanity its unique status is not something humans are but something they are appointed to do. The dignity is positional and functional, not intrinsic — which means it is also recoverable if lost.

        These are claims that have been read into the text from later philosophical sources — Platonic, Aristotelian, and Stoic concepts absorbed into Christian and Jewish theology during the Hellenistic period and beyond. To read Bereshit (Genesis) in its own Ancient Near Eastern context is to recover a vision of adam (humanity) that is at once more concrete and more exalted than the philosophical tradition has often allowed: not beings with souls temporarily housed in bodies, but living tselem-s — animated statues of the Creator, placed on Earth to administer a realm destined to become a sanctuary, commissioned in the presence of the Divine Council, and charged with a task whose recovery drives the entire biblical narrative.

        What Bereshit D1 says about humanity is functional, relational, and administrative: ha-adam is made in tselem, after demut, in order to yirdu and kivshuha. The purpose is the image; the image is the purpose. Once that is heard, the rest of the Bible reads differently — and the question "What are human beings for?" receives an answer rooted not in philosophical speculation but in the opening decree of the cosmic King, publicly issued before the governing powers of heaven.
